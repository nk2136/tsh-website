Six months after an engineer starts at a client, we call them. Just a phone call. Twenty, maybe thirty minutes. No agenda we'd write down. We ask how it's going.

This is not a survey. We don't have a Likert scale. We don't ask them to score anything from one to ten. We've tried it both ways over the years, and the spreadsheet version always tells us less than the conversation does. The thing we're listening for at the six-month mark is whatever the engineer didn't say at week one — the quieter, slower truth that only shows up after the new-job adrenaline wears off and the actual texture of the role settles in.

## What we hear, again and again

The pattern that surprised us most, early on, is how rarely engineers volunteer the headline complaints we'd predicted. Compensation almost never comes up first. Tools come up sometimes. The thing that comes up most often, when we leave enough silence for it, is *team*.

Specifically: how lonely the work is, or isn't. How much the manager actually says in 1:1s. Whether the engineer has anyone they consider a peer — someone they can ask a half-formed question to without rehearsing it first. The presence or absence of that one peer is the single best predictor we've found of whether the engineement will last past month twelve. We know this not because we modeled it but because we kept a running tally on an index card pinned above one of our desks for two years. The card was right.

## Things we hear that we don't expect

Some of the small details are nearly poetic in their specificity. *The standup is at 8:30 my time and I'm in Eastern, so I'm always the one who never has coffee yet.* *My laptop is from 2019 and the battery doesn't hold a charge, but I'm afraid asking for a new one will look needy.* *The senior engineer on my team is brilliant and never replies in the channel, only in DMs, and I never know if I'm doing it right.*

> The thing the engineer didn't say in week one is, almost always, the thing that decides whether they're still there in year two.

These are not the kinds of things a placement firm typically hears. Most engagements end at the closing call. We've made it our practice not to. The six-month check-in costs us roughly one calendar slot per engineer. It pays for itself many times over in retention, in referrals from happy candidates, in the texture of trust we end up building with people we hope to place again in five or ten years.

## What we do with what we learn

We almost never escalate to the client without permission. The whole exercise dies if the engineer thinks they're being reported on. So mostly we just listen, and we coach. *Ask for the laptop. Frame it like this. Bring it up in the next 1:1.* If something is structurally broken — a manager who isn't managing, a project that has been quietly cancelled, a comp expectation that was sandbagged — we may, with the engineer's blessing, raise it through our client-side relationship. Usually we don't have to. Most of the time, simply naming the thing is enough for the engineer to act on it themselves.

Occasionally we hear something at the six-month call that genuinely changes how we work. A few years ago, an engineer told us, almost in passing, that she'd been hesitant to mention a serious health issue during her interview process because she didn't trust the recruiter to keep it private. She told us at six months because by then she trusted *us*. We tightened our intake practice immediately. There is a version of that story we don't think we'd ever have heard if we hadn't called back.

## Why it matters more than it should

The honest answer is that staffing is full of small kindnesses that don't scale and aren't billable. We've decided to do them anyway. The six-month check-in is one. There are others. None of them show up on a sales deck. All of them, taken together, are most of what we mean when we say we do this work *deliberately*.

If you're an engineer we've placed and your six-month mark is coming up, expect a call. We have nothing to sell you. We just want to know how it actually went.
