The first time a candidate asks what our cut is, something small and good happens in the room. They've decided we're worth the awkward question. We try to deserve it.

Most staffing firms treat the pay/bill spread — the gap between what the client pays and what the engineer takes home — like a state secret. We've never understood the logic. The client knows the bill rate. The engineer, eventually, learns the pay rate. Anyone with a pen and a spare minute can do the subtraction. Pretending otherwise just buys you a few months of opacity in exchange for a long arc of mistrust. That isn't a trade we're interested in.

So here, in plain language, is what actually sits between those two numbers.

## The fixed costs that nobody talks about

When an engineer is on a W-2 contract through TSH, *we* are their employer of record. That means we carry a stack of obligations the engineer would otherwise eat themselves on a 1099 — and that the client never sees on the invoice. Employer-side payroll taxes run roughly 7.65% of pay. State unemployment insurance is another bracketed slice. Workers' comp, depending on geography and classification, a smaller one. Then there's general liability, professional liability, and a small reserve for the inevitable week where a timesheet bounces or a benefits invoice comes due before a client check clears.

None of these numbers are dramatic. Together they're rarely glamorous. But they exist, and they are paid every cycle, and they are part of why the spread is not a slush fund.

## The recruiting cost, honestly accounted

The other big chunk is the work that gets the engineer into the seat in the first place. A senior recruiter, doing the job the way we want it done, costs us money. So does the time we spend prepping engineers for interviews — that's typically two or three calls per submission, sometimes more. So does the time we spend representing them after placement when something at the client gets weird. We bake all of that into the spread because we'd rather it be inside the rate than outside it as a surprise fee.

> A staffing firm that won't tell you what it's charging is, almost by definition, charging you for not telling you.

This is the line we keep coming back to.

## What the spread is *not*

It's not a coupon for the client. It's not a hidden bonus to the recruiter on top of their salary. It's not, in our case, an extraction. It's the price of a very specific service: matching, vetting, employing, prepping, and standing behind an engineer through the arc of an engagement. When we run the numbers honestly, the net margin after all the costs above is — frankly — modest. Healthy enough to keep the lights on and the team paid. Not extravagant.

Some firms run the same equation and arrive at extravagant anyway. They get there by trimming the recruiting investment, skipping the prep calls, ignoring the engineer after week one, and pocketing the difference. The math works for a quarter or two. Then the engineers start to talk to each other.

## The transparent rate conversation

In practice, this is what an honest rate conversation looks like at TSH. We tell the candidate the bill rate the client has approved. We tell them the pay rate we're proposing. If they ask what's in between, we walk them through it: roughly so much for taxes, so much for insurance, so much for the fixed cost of having TSH stand behind the engagement. And then a margin. We name the margin. It's usually somewhere between 12 and 22 percent of the bill rate, depending on the role, the geography, and whether we sourced through a vendor partner.

We don't pretend the margin is zero. We don't apologize for it either. We worked for it. We continue to work for it after the engineer starts.

## Why this is good for the client too

Here's the part that surprises people. Clients who learn that we're transparent with our engineers tend to relax — not stiffen. They've been buying staffing services long enough to know the alternative. The engineer who feels squeezed shows up squeezed. The engineer who feels seen shows up clear. Year-two retention for engineers placed through transparent firms is materially better than the industry mean. We don't have a perfect study to cite, just six years of running the experiment ourselves.

The pay/bill spread is not a number to be ashamed of. It's a number to be *earned*. We'd rather you know what it is than wonder.
